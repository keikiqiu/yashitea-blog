If I had to pick one tea to drink for the rest of my life, it would be Mi Lan Xiang.
There are dozens of Dan Cong varieties, and I have tried most of them. But Honey Orchid keeps pulling me back. Sweet but not cloying, floral but not perfumey, smooth but complex enough for fifteen infusions.
I first tried it at a small tea shop in Guangzhou. The owner brewed it silently and pushed a tiny cup toward me. Smell first, she said. I lifted the cup. The fragrance hit me like a garden in bloom  jasmine, honey, warm sunlight.
The tea was so smooth it felt like drinking liquid silk. The sweetness came first, then floral notes, then a hint of fruit. The finish was long and clean. This is Mi Lan Xiang, she said. The queen of Dan Cong. I bought 200 grams that day.
Mi Lan Xiang gets its name from mi (honey) and lan (orchid). The honey note comes from the plants natural nectar, concentrated by high-altitude growing. The orchid note develops during oxidation.
Not all Mi Lan Xiang is equal. Cheap versions taste flat. Good Mi Lan Xiang has layers: first honey, then flowers, then stone fruit, then a mineral finish that reminds me of mountain rocks after rain. The aftertaste of great Mi Lan Xiang changes how your mouth feels for an hour.
If you are looking for a Dan Cong to fall in love with, start here.
