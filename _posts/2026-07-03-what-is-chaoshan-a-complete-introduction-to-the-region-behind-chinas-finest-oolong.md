Nestled along the eastern coast of Guangdong Province, Chaoshan is not a city you will find on a standard map. It is something rarer  a cultural region, a linguistic territory, and a state of mind that has shaped Chinese tea culture for over a millennium. To speak of Phoenix Dan Cong without understanding Chaoshan is like discussing Bordeaux without mentioning France.
The name Chaoshan is a blend of two ancient cities: Chaozhou () and Shantou (). Together with the counties of Raoping (), Jieyang (), and Chenghai (), this small coastal triangle has given the world some of Chinas most treasured cultural exports: the exquisite Gongfu tea ceremony, the legendary Phoenix Dan Cong oolong, and a cuisine that ranks among the finest on earth.
What makes Chaoshan unique is its remarkable cultural continuity. While much of China underwent dramatic transformations, Chaoshans relative geographic isolation preserved ancient traditions that have faded elsewhere. The language spoken here, Teochew (), preserves pronunciation from ancient Chinese that modern Mandarin has long lost. And the tea ceremony performed daily in every Chaoshan home traces its roots directly to the Song Dynasty.
For the people of Chaoshan, tea is not a luxury  it is a daily necessity, as fundamental as rice. The local saying goes:   better to go three days without rice than one day without tea. In this series, we take you deep into the heart of Chaoshan  its history, its people, its tea mountains, and the culture that made Phoenix Dan Cong one of the worlds most revered teas.
