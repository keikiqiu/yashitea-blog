Let me tell you about the hardest hike I have ever taken  and the best cup of tea I have ever had.
I arrived at the foot of Phoenix Mountain in Chaozhou at six in the morning. The mountain was so high I could not see the top. Mist covered everything like a white blanket. I looked up and thought, There is no way I am going to make it.
But I started walking anyway. The path was narrow, just dirt and rocks worn smooth by farmers who have walked this route for hundreds of years. After thirty minutes, my legs started burning. After an hour, sweat was dripping down my face so hard I could barely see.
That is when I saw them  the tea terraces. Row after row of dark green bushes planted along the mountainside. The leaves were so vibrant, so alive, they almost glowed. I reached out and touched one. It was soft and thick, covered in tiny hairs. I wanted to kiss it. When you climb that high and see those plants thriving in the mist, you feel something you cannot describe.
A farmer named Mr. Chen was picking leaves nearby. He smiled at me  probably laughing at the out-of-shape guy covered in sweat  and handed me a fresh leaf. Chew it, he said. I put it in my mouth. The taste exploded: first bitter, then sweet, then floral, like jasmine and honey mixed together. I had never tasted anything like it.
This is why our tea is special, Mr. Chen said. The mountain gives us altitude, the fog gives us moisture, the soil gives us minerals. Everything works together.
I spent the morning watching him pick. His hands moved so fast  just a quick snap and each perfect leaf landed in his basket. Two leaves and a bud. Three thousand picks make one pound of tea.
If you ever get the chance to visit Phoenix Mountain, take it. The climb is hard. You will sweat. Your legs will hurt. But when you see the tea gardens with your own eyes, you will understand why Phoenix Dan Cong tastes like nothing else on earth.
