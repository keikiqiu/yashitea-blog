It sounded like a great idea. Fifteen different Dan Cong teas, one afternoon, a notebook, and a lot of hot water.
By tea number 8, I was dizzy. By tea number 12, I could barely tell the difference between honey orchid and almond. My tongue felt raw. I had to take a 30-minute nap in the middle.
But I learned more that afternoon than in months of casual drinking.
1. The differences between aromas are real but subtle. Mi Lan Xiang really smells like honey and flowers. Xing Ren Xiang has a nutty, marzipan quality. Ya Shi Xiang is surprisingly sweet and mineral. Side by side, the differences are clear.
2. Roast level changes everything. Light-roast Mi Lan Xiang is golden and floral. Heavy-roast is amber and toasty. They taste like completely different teas.
3. Your palate gets tired. After 10 teas, everything tasted the same. I should have cleansed my palate with water and crackers. Big mistake.
4. The best tea was the cheapest. The most expensive was $80 for 100g. It was good. But the one I enjoyed most was a $15 basic Dan Cong from a small vendor. Sometimes expensive tea is more interesting than delicious.
5. Water temperature matters hugely. I accidentally brewed one at 100C instead of 90C  bitter and harsh. Same tea at 90C was smooth and sweet. If your Dan Cong tastes bitter, lower the temperature by 5 degrees.
Would I taste 15 teas again? No. 8 is the max. But I am glad I did it once.
