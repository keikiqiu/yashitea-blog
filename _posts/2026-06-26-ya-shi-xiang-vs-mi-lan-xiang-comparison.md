I have spent a lot of time with both of these teas, and the honest answer is: it depends on what you are looking for. Here is a side-by-side comparison based on my own tasting experience.

Let us get the name out of the way first. Ya Shi Xiang translates to Duck Shit Aroma, and I will admit, the first time I heard it I was skeptical. But the story behind the name is worth knowing: a farmer in Chaozhou discovered this exceptional tea bush and wanted to keep its location secret. When people asked about it, he said it grew in a duck pen (ya shi). The name stuck, and today it is one of the most sought-after teas in China.

In reality, the aroma is nothing short of spectacular. Imagine the most fragrant orchid you can think of, mixed with jasmine and a hint of honey. The liquor is bright golden  almost glowing  and the taste is remarkably smooth. There is a creaminess to it that I have rarely found in other oolongs.

Profile: Floral-forward, silky, elegant
Roast: Medium (light enough to preserve the fragrance, dark enough to add depth)
Origin: 900m+ elevation, Fenghuang Mountain
Best for: People who love floral aromas and delicate flavors

Mi Lan Xiang translates to Honey Orchid Fragrance, and it delivers exactly what the name promises. This is the tea I recommend to anyone who is trying Dan Cong for the first time. Why? Because it is more forgiving and its sweetness is immediately accessible.

The honey note in Mi Lan Xiang is not subtle  it hits you right away. There is a thickness to the liquor, almost like nectar, and the orchid fragrance weaves through it beautifully. This tea has a heavier roast than Ya Shi Xiang, giving it a comforting, toasty character underneath the sweetness.

Profile: Sweet, full-bodied, honeyed
Roast: Medium-heavy (more charcoal character)
Origin: 800m elevation, Fenghuang Mountain
Best for: People who prefer bolder, sweeter, more substantial teas

Start with Mi Lan Xiang if you are new to Dan Cong. Its sweetness is immediately rewarding, and it is more forgiving if your steeping technique is not perfect yet. It also pairs beautifully with food  try it with dark chocolate or dried fruit.

Try Ya Shi Xiang next if you want to understand what the hype is about. It requires a bit more attention when brewing, but the floral complexity it offers is unlike anything else in the tea world. It is the tea I reach for when I want to sit quietly and pay attention.

Buy both if you can. They complement each other beautifully. Ya Shi Xiang for quiet mornings, Mi Lan Xiang for afternoons when you need something comforting. I keep both in my cabinet at all times.

Whichever you choose, buy from a reputable seller. Look for harvest date (spring 2025 or later), specific origin information, and clear roast level labeling. Avoid anything that does not specify which mountain or village it came from. Authentic Dan Cong comes from Fenghuang Mountain  if the label just says China, be cautious.

Ya Shi Xiang has a more intense orchid aroma with a bold, complex flavor profile. Mi Lan Xiang is sweeter with honey notes and a smoother, more approachable taste.

Mi Lan Xiang (Honey Orchid) is better for beginners due to its sweeter, more approachable flavor. Ya Shi Xiang (Duck Shit Aroma) is recommended for experienced oolong drinkers.

Despite its unusual name, Ya Shi Xiang has nothing to do with duck manure. The name comes from the tea's growing environment under certain trees that attracted ducks, and the unique orchid-like aroma it produces after roasting.
