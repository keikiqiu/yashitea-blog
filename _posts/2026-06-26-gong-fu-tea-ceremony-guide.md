Gong Fu tea ceremony is a traditional Chinese method of brewing tea that emphasizes precision, mindfulness, and multiple short infusions. Using small clay teapots and tiny cups, Gong Fu (meaning "skill" or "effort") extracts the full flavor potential of high-quality teas like Dan Cong oolong.

            
        

The first time I experienced a proper Chaozhou Gong Fu tea ceremony, I was nervous. The tea master moved with such precision and grace that I was afraid to touch anything. But here is the secret: Gong Fu is not about being formal. It is about focusing your attention on the tea, and everything else follows naturally.

What Gong Fu Actually Means

Gong Fu () translates to skill achieved through hard work. It is the same word used for martial arts. In tea, it refers to the practice of brewing tea with careful attention to every detail  water temperature, steeping time, vessel selection, pouring technique.

Gong Fu tea originated in the Chaozhou and Shantou region of Guangdong  the same region that produces Dan Cong. This is not a coincidence. The two traditions evolved together, each influencing the other. Dan Congs complex flavors demand the precision of Gong Fu brewing, and Gong Fu brewing exists to showcase teas like Dan Cong.

Essential Equipment

You do not need much to start. Here is the minimal setup:

Gaiwan ()  a lidded bowl, typically 100150ml. The most versatile brewing vessel. Porcelain is best for beginners because it does not absorb flavors.
Cha Hai ()  a fairness pitcher. After steeping, you pour the tea into this before serving, ensuring every cup has the same strength.
Gong Dao Bei ()  tasting cups. Small cups (3050ml) that allow you to appreciate the teas color, aroma, and flavor.
Tea boat or tray  to catch spilled water. A simple baking tray works fine to start.

The Ritual, Step by Step

1. Warm the Vessels

Pour boiling water into the gaiwan, then into the cups. This cleans and warms everything, helping the tea brew evenly.

2. Add the Tea

Put 58g of Dan Cong into the warm gaiwan. Inhale the aroma of the heated leaves  this is called dry fragrance and it tells you a lot about the teas quality.

3. Rinse

Pour water over the leaves and immediately discard. This wakes up the leaves. The rinse water is not meant to be drunk.

            
        

4. Brew and Serve

Fill the gaiwan with boiling water. Steep for 1015 seconds for the first brew. Pour into the fairness pitcher, then into individual cups. Serve with both hands as a sign of respect.

5. Repeat

Add 510 seconds for each subsequent brew. Good Dan Cong will give 812+ infusions. Each one tastes slightly different  that is the beauty of Gong Fu brewing.
