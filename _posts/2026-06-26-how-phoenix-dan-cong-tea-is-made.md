Phoenix Dan Cong tea is made through an intricate 8-step process: withering (sun-drying), cooling, tossing (bruising to initiate oxidation), oxidation, fixing (pan-firing to stop oxidation), rolling, shaping, and roasting. The entire process takes 20-30 hours and requires skilled tea masters.

            
        

The first time I watched a tea master make Dan Cong, I was struck by how physically demanding the process is. It is not a gentle, romantic affair  it is hot, sweaty work that requires split-second timing and decades of experience. Here is how those incredible flavors end up in your cup.

Step 1: Harvesting

Dan Cong is harvested in flushes throughout the growing season. The spring harvest (MarchApril) is the most prized  the leaves are tender, packed with nutrients from the winter dormancy, and rich in aromatic compounds.

Harvesting is done entirely by hand. Pickers select specific leaf combinations depending on the desired quality: one bud with one leaf for the finest grades, one bud with two or three leaves for standard grades. A skilled picker can harvest about 1015kg of fresh leaves per day.

Step 2: Withering (Sun Drying)

Freshly picked leaves are spread thinly on bamboo trays and placed in the sun for 3060 minutes. This removes surface moisture and makes the leaves pliable. The withering time depends on weather conditions  too much sun and the leaves dry out, too little and they do not soften enough.

On rainy days, tea masters use indoor withering rooms with controlled airflow. It is less ideal than sun withering, but sometimes necessary.

Step 3: Cooling and Tossing (The Crucial Step)

After sun withering, the leaves are brought indoors and spread out to cool. Then begins the most important step: the tossing (zuo qing). The leaves are gently tossed and turned by hand in bamboo trays, bruising the leaf edges slightly to initiate oxidation.

This is where the magic happens. The bruising causes the leaf cells to break down, starting chemical reactions that produce the teas characteristic aromas. The tea master must judge exactly how much oxidation is needed  too little and the tea will be grassy, too much and it will lose its floral character. For Dan Cong, target oxidation is typically 1530%.

Step 4: Fixation (Kill Green)

Once the desired oxidation level is reached, the leaves are quickly heated in large woks or drums at high temperature (200250C). This fixes the oxidation by deactivating the enzymes, locking in the flavor profile the tea master has crafted.

            
        

Step 5: Rolling

The fixed leaves are rolled into their characteristic twisted shapes. Rolling also breaks down cell walls further, releasing juices that will contribute to the teas flavor during brewing. For Dan Cong, the leaves are typically rolled into long, twisted strips rather than the tightly balled shape of Tie Guan Yin.
