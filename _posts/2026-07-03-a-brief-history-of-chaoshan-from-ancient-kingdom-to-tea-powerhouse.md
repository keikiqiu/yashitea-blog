The story of Chaoshan begins over two thousand years ago, when this coastal region was part of the ancient Nanyue Kingdom. But its transformation into a tea powerhouse began during the Tang Dynasty (618907 CE), when settlers from central China brought advanced agricultural techniques  and the tea plant.
The Song Dynasty Golden Age. During the Song Dynasty (9601279), Chaozhou flourished. The imperial court was obsessed with tea  Emperor Huizong himself wrote a tea treatise. When the Song court fell to Mongol invaders in 1279, scholars and officials fled south, finding refuge in remote Chaozhou. They brought their tea culture, which the local people preserved even as it disappeared from the rest of China.
The Ming Transformation. During the Ming Dynasty, loose-leaf tea replaced powdered tea across China. In Chaozhou, this gave birth to Gongfu tea  brewing small amounts of high-quality leaves in small clay pots with precise temperature control and multiple short infusions.
Rise of Phoenix Dan Cong. On Phoenix Mountain, farmers discovered that certain wild tea trees produced extraordinary aromas. By selecting and propagating these individual single bush trees, they created over a hundred distinct aroma profiles.
Today. Phoenix Dan Cong commands premium prices globally, yet it remains unchanged  still produced by small family farms on Phoenix Mountain slopes, using techniques perfected over generations.
