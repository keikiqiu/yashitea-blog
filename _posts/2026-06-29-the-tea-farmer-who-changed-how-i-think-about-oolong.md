His name is Uncle Lin. He is 67. He has been making tea since he was 12. His hands are covered in calluses and scars from decades of work. When he shakes your hand, his grip is strong but his palms are surprisingly soft  soft from handling tea leaves every day for 55 years.
I met Uncle Lin during my second visit to Phoenix Mountain. He makes the best Mi Lan Xiang in the village, my friend said. I nodded politely, not really believing it.
But then Uncle Lin brewed me a cup. And I understood.
He did not use a gaiwan. He used a small Chaozhou clay pot, dark red and seasoned from years of use. This pot has drunk more tea than you have, he joked. He rinsed the pot, added leaves, poured water, and poured out the first steep in three seconds.
Taste, he said.
I lifted the tiny cup. The tea was different. Smoother. Sweeter. The honey note was so strong it felt like liquid flowers. I closed my eyes. For a moment, I forgot where I was.
He has a garden at 900 meters with volcanic soil. Some of his bushes are over 60 years old. Old trees have deeper roots. They find more flavor in the ground.
He showed me his roasting room  a small concrete space with a charcoal fire pit. The smoke smell was incredible: woody, sweet, warm. I roast for 8 hours, rest for 2 days, then roast again. Three times.
I bought 500 grams of his Mi Lan Xiang. When I brewed it at home, each infusion revealed a new layer. Honey. Flowers. Spice. A finish that lasted minutes.
I still think about that afternoon in Uncle Lins kitchen  the old man shrugging like his magic was nothing special. To me, it was a lesson in what tea can be when made with love.
