Most Dan Cong drinkers start with Mi Lan Xiang or Ya Shi Xiang. I started with Song Zhong, by accident, and it ruined me for all other teas.
A tea friend handed me a bag. Try this. It tastes like pine trees and rocks. I was skeptical. Tea that tastes like a forest? I had to try it.
Song Zhong means Pine Varietal. The tea bushes were originally planted among pine trees on Phoenix Mountain. Over generations, the plants absorbed the mineral character of the soil where pine needles decomposed. The result is a tea that genuinely tastes like a mountain forest.
The first steep was unlike any tea I had tried. The aroma was woody and herbal  pine needles, moss, wet stones. The taste was savory rather than sweet, umami like a light broth. There was a slight astringency like pine bark, but pleasant, like fresh mountain air.
Song Zhong is believed to be one of the oldest Dan Cong varieties. Some original bushes are over 200 years old. The flavor is not as immediately appealing as sweet, fruity Dan Congs. It takes time to appreciate. But once you develop the taste, other teas feel almost too simple.
I drink Song Zhong on rainy days. Something about the mineral, forest-like flavor matches gray sky and rain on the roof. It is a contemplative tea.
Frequently Asked Questions
Q: Does Song Zhong really taste like pine?A: Yes, subtly. The dominant notes are woody, mineral, and herbal.
Q: How do you brew Song Zhong?A: 5g per 100ml gaiwan, 95C, 10-second steeps. It handles more infusions than most Dan Congs.
