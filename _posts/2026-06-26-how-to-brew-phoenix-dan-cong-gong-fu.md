To brew Phoenix Dan Cong Gong Fu style: use 5g of tea leaves per 100ml water at 205F (96C). Rinse leaves first, then steep for 10-15 seconds for the first infusion. Each subsequent infusion add 5-10 seconds. A good Dan Cong can produce 10-15 infusions.

The first time someone showed me how to brew Dan Cong Gong Fu style, I thought it was unnecessarily complicated. Boil water, warm the cups, rinse the leaves, short steeps, pour into a fairness pitcher, serve in tiny cups It felt like a lot of fuss for a cup of tea.

Brewing Dan Cong Gong Fu style is not about being fancy. It is about getting the best possible flavor out of exceptional tea leaves. Here is the method I use every day, explained simply.

You do not need a full Chinese tea ceremony set to brew good Dan Cong. Here is the minimum:

A gaiwan (150ml)  a lidded bowl, essentially. Cheap ones cost $10$15 and work perfectly.
58g of Dan Cong  about one and a half to two teaspoons.
Boiling water  100C. Not 90C, not 95C. Boiling.
A small pitcher or cup to pour the tea into before serving.

That is it. No fancy Yixing teapot required (though they do improve the experience over time).

Pour boiling water into your gaiwan, then pour it into your cups. This serves two purposes: it cleans everything, and it preheats the vessel so your tea stays hot during brewing. Discard the water.

Put 58g of Dan Cong into the warm gaiwan. Immediately smell the dry leaves  the heat from the gaiwan will release some of the aroma. With a good Mi Lan Xiang, you should already catch honey and orchid notes at this stage.

Pour hot water over the leaves and pour it out immediately within 35 seconds. This washes the tea and wakes up the leaves. Some people say the rinse is just for cleaning, but honestly, the real reason is that it prepares the leaves to release their full flavor in the next steep. The aroma of the wet leaves after the rinse is one of the best parts of the experience.

Fill the gaiwan with boiling water, cover it, and steep for exactly 1015 seconds. Pour into your pitcher or directly into cups. Taste it. Notice the color  it should be bright and clear, not cloudy.

Infusions 12: Light, aromatic, the leaves are just opening.
Infusions 35: The sweet spot. Full flavor, complex aroma.
Infusions 68: The flavor evolves, new notes emerge.
Infusions 912: Gentle sweetness, the tea winding down gracefully.

A good Dan Cong will easily give you 10+ satisfying infusions. That means one session can last an entire afternoon.

Q: My Dan Cong tastes bitter. What went wrong?
A: Most likely one of three things: water too cold (yes, too cold can make it flat and bitter), steeped too long (stick to 1015 seconds for early brews), or too much leaf. Try reducing the leaf amount slightly.

Q: Can I brew it western style in a large teapot?
A: You can, but you will lose a lot of the complexity. Gong Fu style uses more leaf and shorter steeps, which extracts a wider range of flavors. Western style with 3g in a large pot for 3 minutes will give you a pleasant but one-dimensional cup.

Q: How do I know when the leaves are spent?
A: When the flavor becomes noticeably thin and watery, usually after 1012 infusions. Good leaves will still be fragrant even when spent  you can smell the wet leaves in the gaiwan and often catch aromas you missed during brewing.

The Gong Fu method takes a bit of practice, but it quickly becomes second nature. After a few sessions, you will find yourself reaching for your gaiwan automatically. The ritual becomes meditative  something I genuinely look forward to at the end of a long day.

Start with a decent Mi Lan Xiang, follow these steps, and I promise you will taste things in Dan Cong that you never knew were there.
